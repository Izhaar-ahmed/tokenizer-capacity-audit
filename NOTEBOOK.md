# Notebook

Rough chronological log of how this actually went, including the parts that didn't work. I'm keeping the dead ends in on purpose, the assignment's pretty clear that a notebook with none is a red flag.

## Part A

First thing I did, before touching a single line of code, was just run `given/fertility.py` as is against the sample corpora and make sure I got the same numbers the report quotes. Eng 1.27, hin 7.45, ratio 5.89x, matches. Good, now I've got something to diff every fix against instead of just trusting the report.

Tried to get tiktoken to fetch the GPT-2 vocab the normal way and it just wouldn't connect, same for pulling FLORES-200 directly off GitHub or HuggingFace. Spent a good twenty minutes convincing myself this was an actual block and not just a flaky connection, tried a few different hosts, a couple of different libraries, no luck. Ended up finding that `openai-whisper` on PyPI (which was reachable) ships the exact GPT-2 BPE rank table as one of its bundled assets, plus a genuinely multilingual byte-BPE tokenizer as a bonus. Used both of those. Wrote `tok_common.py` so that if you've got normal internet, it just calls `tiktoken.get_encoding()` like anyone would, and only falls back to the whisper package trick if that fails.

Then read through `fertility.py` properly looking for what's actually wrong with it. Three things jumped out as possible code bugs, the `split(" ")` call, the `.lower()` call, and how it averages fertility across lines, and two things that looked a little odd but I wasn't sure about yet, the unused `random.seed`, and the NFC normalization.

Built `bug_isolation.py` to test each of these one at a time rather than guessing. A few things worth noting from that:

The double space bug was fun to find, both toy corpora have a planted double space in them (eng sample line 7, hindi sample line 10), which is exactly what `split(" ")` trips over. Confirmed it directly, that one line counts as 8 words with `split(" ")` and 7 with `split()`.

The lowercasing one actually surprised me, and I want to be honest about that instead of pretending I called it correctly from the start. My gut assumption was that lowercasing would make English look worse than it should, throwing away casing information seemed like it'd hurt compression. I checked the actual token counts before writing anything down and got the opposite, lowering the English text added tokens (96 to 99), because GPT-2's BPE has separate learned merges for capitalized forms and you lose those when you lowercase. Hindi didn't move at all, no case distinction to lose. So the bug actually makes the report's ratio look smaller than the truth, not bigger. Good thing I checked instead of trusting my first instinct.

The per-line averaging one took me longer to actually understand than I expected, so I sat with it for a bit rather than skimming past it. The script computes tokens/words separately for each line and then averages those per-line ratios, which sounds fine until you realize a 3-word line and a 40-word line count equally in that average even though the 40-word line represents way more of the corpus. The right way is to sum tokens and sum words across the whole thing first, then divide once. On the 10-line toy corpus this barely moves anything (5.89 to 5.91) because the sample lines are all roughly the same length, so it's easy to look at that tiny delta and dismiss it. I don't think that's the right read though, the size of the bias depends entirely on how much the line lengths vary, and real chat traffic varies a lot more than ten sample sentences do. I fixed it in the corrected script for that reason, even though I'm not going to pretend the toy-corpus evidence for it is dramatic.

For the two "suspicious but fine" candidates, checked `random` usage across the whole file with a grep and it's called exactly zero times outside the import and the seed line, so that one's just dead code, no experiment needed. NFC normalization I actually tested, none of the ten Hindi sample lines change under it, confirming it's not doing anything harmful here, and it's genuinely good practice for real world text that might come in with inconsistent Unicode encoding. Neither of these is a bug, I'm fairly confident about that and don't think it needs more hedging than that.

At this point I had a decent case that the interesting problem wasn't in the code at all, it was the choice of tokens per word as the thing to measure in the first place. Agglutinative languages pack more meaning into a single word, so the denominator is comparing apples to oranges across languages. To actually test that I needed a real multilingual corpus, which is where A1 comes in.

This is where I lost the most time. Tried several HuggingFace mirrors of FLORES-200, `facebook/flores`, `Muennighoff/flores200`, `mteb/flores`, a couple of others, through the datasets-server API, since direct file downloads were blocked but that API wasn't. `mteb/flores` technically works but every row comes back with roughly 200 languages crammed into one JSON blob, which makes pulling out just the six languages I needed painfully slow. The other two mirrors don't have a working row viewer at all. At one point I handed the search off to try to parallelize it, and that ran into its own snag partway through before I picked it back up myself and found that `Helsinki-NLP/opus-100` has clean per-language-pair configs that just return `{en, xx}` pairs directly, no mess. Switched to that, and documented clearly in `corpus/PROVENANCE.md` that this is OPUS-100, not FLORES-200, and why.

Then hit another snag with Kannada specifically, the `en-kn` split in OPUS-100 turned out to be almost entirely GNOME and KDE desktop translation strings ("Minimize", "& Insert," that kind of thing), not real sentences. Checked the train split too in case test was just unlucky, same story. Ended up with only 8 usable Kannada sentences after filtering out the junk, against 68 for Hindi. I thought about padding it out with lower quality data just to make the n look better, decided against it, and wrote it up honestly instead, turns out that's actually a decent finding on its own, Kannada is just less represented in general bitext than the other Dravidian languages here.

Telugu is my first language, so once I had a working corpus I went back and actually read through `tel.tsv` myself instead of just trusting that OPUS-100's alignment was correct. This turned out to matter. A handful of the pairs that survived my filtering (dropping obvious UI strings and translator credits) are still bad Telugu, scrambled word order, words run together where there should be spaces, the kind of thing you'd only catch if you actually speak the language. One example that stuck with me, a line about high school friends comes out with "I" and "we" sitting right next to each other in a way nobody would actually write in Telugu. I left those lines in rather than cleaning them out further, and wrote it up as its own caveat in `PROVENANCE.md`, because it's a genuinely different problem from Kannada's. Kannada doesn't have enough real sentences. Telugu has plenty of sentences, but some meaningful fraction of them aren't trustworthy as "natural Telugu." I don't think this shows up unless you actually read the language, which is exactly why I think it's worth including rather than assuming every language's data is equally clean.

Once the corpus was in decent shape, ran the corrected analysis with two tokenizers and got what I was expecting to see, more or less: swapping gpt2 for a multilingual tokenizer dropped Tamil's per sentence ratio from 15.9x to 4.9x, Hindi from 6.9x to 4.7x. Also checked gpt2's tok/byte on Tamil specifically and got 0.996, basically one token per byte, which is about as clean a signature of byte level fallback as you could ask for. That number ended up being the strongest single piece of evidence against the report's "it's the script's fault" claim.

Went back later and grew the corpus, first pass only had a dozen or two sentences per language, and a memo recommending a cost multiplier shouldn't be resting on that thin a sample. Pulled more rows, added Telugu and Malayalam on top of Tamil and Kannada (the assignment only asked for two Dravidian languages, I used all four I could get reasonable data for), then went back a second time and pulled more Telugu specifically once I started reading it closely, re-ran everything each time. The numbers shifted by a few percent, nothing changed direction. Worth mentioning that in the defense, the conclusion held up when I doubled the amount of evidence behind it.

## Part B

Wanted the KV-cache math to at least roughly predict what the log shows before trusting either one. First pass at the bytes-per-token formula, I used 24 heads, the attention head count listed in the spec. The predicted max-concurrency number came out way off from what the log implies, and going back to `model_spec.md` I noticed it separately lists "attention heads (Q): 24" and "KV heads (GQA): 8", this model uses grouped-query attention, so the KV cache is sized by the 8 KV heads, not the 24 query heads. That's the moment in this whole assignment where reading the spec more carefully actually mattered. Redid it with 8 heads, got 112 KiB/token, predicted about 29 max sequences, which lines up with what the log implies (about 26) to within roughly 10 percent.

For the throughput anomaly, I didn't want to just eyeball it and guess, pulled up the `preempted_seqs` and `kv_cache_util` columns side by side. Batch 24 sits at 0.93 util with zero preemptions, batch 32 and 48 both pin at 0.97 with 7 and 23 preemptions. That's the mechanism, right there in the columns, no speculation needed.

The `reported_tok_s` thing started from a gut check, the report says batch 48 should hit around 3200 tok/s, and that number felt physically wrong given the GPU only has 300 GB/s of bandwidth and 8.4 GB of weights to push through, back of envelope, that's around 36 forward passes a second as a hard ceiling, nowhere near enough headroom for another 2x jump on top of what's already reported at batch 24. So I tried reconstructing the column directly instead of guessing at what it might be counting: `n * (prompt_len + gen_len) / wall_clock`. Matched the reported number on all 13 rows in the log to within a tenth of a percent. That's not a coincidence, it's counting prompt tokens and generated tokens together, which is a completely different thing from delivered throughput.
